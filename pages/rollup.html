What It Is

Defining the Roll-Up

A roll-up acquisition strategy involves the systematic purchase of multiple companies in the same or adjacent industries, combining them under a single platform to achieve scale, operational efficiency, and market positioning unavailable to any individual operator. The strategy is distinct from conglomerate diversification in a critical way: the acquirer deliberately pursues pure-play concentration rather than portfolio diversification. Every acquisition deepens the position in a single market rather than spreading risk across multiple markets.

The academic literature on roll-ups identifies two primary mechanisms of value creation: operational synergies and multiple expansion. In practice, the most powerful roll-up platforms capture both simultaneously. The acquirer buys fragmented operators at single-asset valuation multiples, combines them into a platform valued at a much higher multiple, and then engineers margin expansion by extracting operating leverage from the enlarged cost base.

The strategy demands specific preconditions. The target industry must be genuinely fragmented, meaning no single operator holds dominant market share and the barriers to consolidation are low enough to permit serial acquisitions at reasonable prices. It must be large enough in total addressable market to absorb significant capital deployment without saturating the opportunity. And it must contain identifiable operational inefficiencies that scale can plausibly address.

The core arbitrage: Private market operators in fragmented industries typically sell at 5x to 7x EBITDA. The publicly traded consolidated platform they are absorbed into trades at 12x to 18x EBITDA. Each acquisition at below-platform multiples is immediately value-accretive to the acquiring entity's shareholders, before any operational improvement is considered.

How Value Is Created

Three Compounding Forces

01
Multiple Expansion
Individual operators in fragmented markets trade at low acquisition multiples because they lack scale, management depth, and institutional sponsorship. When absorbed into a publicly traded platform with professional management and access to capital markets, the same cash flows are re-rated at a significantly higher valuation multiple. The arithmetic is direct: buy at 6x EBITDA, trade at 14x EBITDA, and each dollar of acquired EBITDA creates $8 in enterprise value before any operational improvement.
02
Operating Leverage
Fixed costs spread across a larger revenue base drive margin expansion. Scale enables procurement leverage: larger purchasing volumes extract better vendor pricing and terms. Shared services eliminate duplicative administrative overhead across acquired units. Technology investment that would be cost-prohibitive for a small operator becomes economically rational when amortized across a national platform. Each incremental acquisition improves the unit economics of the entire combined business.
03
Revenue Synergies
Geographic reach allows large customers to be served consistently across project locations. Category expansion through complementary acquisitions enables cross-selling into an existing customer base. National contracts unavailable to regional operators become accessible once a platform reaches sufficient geographic density. QXO explicitly cites cross-selling of Kodiak's lumber, windows, and doors into Beacon's roofing and waterproofing contractor base as a near-term revenue opportunity, alongside new access to large homebuilders requiring multi-site supply consistency.
04
Capital Markets Access
Publicly traded platforms access equity and debt capital at a cost unavailable to private operators. QXO raised approximately $5 billion in equity before completing a single acquisition. This pre-funded war chest eliminates the financing uncertainty that slows deal execution and allows the acquirer to move quickly, price competitively, and avoid financing contingencies that reduce deal certainty from the seller's perspective. The ability to offer stock consideration also widens the universe of sellers.
05
Technology as Differentiator
Building products distribution has historically operated on thin technology infrastructure. Digital ordering, AI-powered inventory optimization, and real-time pricing visibility are standard in mature distribution verticals but largely absent from this sector. A well-capitalized platform can deploy technology investment that increases gross margin per transaction, reduces inventory carrying costs, and improves sales force productivity. QXO explicitly frames technology as a core component of its margin expansion thesis, having appointed a Chief AI Officer and partnering with Roofr for digital ordering.
06
Execution Velocity
The roll-up advantage compounds with speed. An acquirer that closes multiple transactions per year builds a larger platform faster, which in turn makes subsequent acquisitions more accretive because the fixed-cost base is already spread across more revenue. Jacobs committed over $13 billion to acquisitions within eleven months of operational control of QXO, a pace that reflects deliberate prioritization of speed of deployment over caution. Each closed transaction adds procurement leverage, customer reach, and management capacity for the next.
The Arithmetic

Multiple Expansion: How the Math Works

The Accretion Equation

Consider a simplified illustration. A regional building products distributor generates $30 million in EBITDA and is acquired at a private market multiple of 6x, implying a purchase price of $180 million. The acquiring public company trades at 14x EBITDA. At the moment of closing, the acquired EBITDA of $30 million is worth $420 million to the acquirer's shareholders at the platform multiple. The acquirer paid $180 million and received $420 million in intrinsic value, creating $240 million in value before accounting for any synergies or operational improvements.

This multiple arbitrage is the mechanical foundation of every roll-up. It works as long as three conditions hold: the acquirer can sustain a higher trading multiple than private market transaction multiples; the acquirer can continue sourcing and executing acquisitions below that trading multiple; and the market continues to credit the acquirer with the platform premium rather than discounting it for integration risk or leverage.

Jacobs addresses the third condition through an operating track record, conservative leverage, and transparent disclosure. His history of successful exits at United Waste and United Rentals, and the compounding returns at XPO, provide the market with a framework for assigning platform multiple credibility to QXO at an early stage.

QXO's Actual Multiples

The Beacon acquisition was priced at $124.35 per share in cash, representing a total enterprise value of approximately $11 billion. QXO's own press release on the TopBuild announcement states that the Beacon-implied EBITDA multiple, using TopBuild's comparable EBITDA as a reference, reflected the discipline of buying at 14.9x before synergies. The Kodiak acquisition at $2.25 billion for a company generating $2.4 billion in revenue implies a revenue multiple below 1.0x, consistent with private market distribution asset pricing.

The Framework

The Jacobs Operating Model

Across four distinct industries, Brad Jacobs has applied a recognizable framework with consistent characteristics. The pattern is not accidental. It is a deliberately engineered repeatable process, and its consistency across dissimilar industries is the strongest evidence that the value creation is driven by method rather than by market conditions or luck.

01
Capital Before Acquisitions
Jacobs consistently raises institutional capital before identifying specific acquisition targets. This inverts the conventional private equity model where capital is committed to a specific investment. Pre-funding eliminates financing contingencies from deal terms, accelerates closing timelines, and allows the acquirer to approach sellers from a position of unconditional financial strength. QXO raised approximately $5 billion before making its first acquisition offer.
02
Industry Selection Criteria
Target industries share specific attributes: large total addressable market (typically $200 billion or more), extreme fragmentation with no dominant operator, low technology penetration creating an identifiable improvement opportunity, and stable underlying demand drivers. Building products distribution satisfies all four. Solid waste, equipment rental, and logistics all satisfied the same criteria at the time Jacobs entered.
03
Platform Acquisition First
The initial transaction establishes the operational platform and management infrastructure. Jacobs invests significant due diligence and premium in the first deal because it defines the foundation for all subsequent acquisitions. Beacon Roofing Supply, at $11 billion, was not a bargain acquisition. It was a premium paid for a national platform with 600 branches, established vendor relationships, and operational management that would run QXO Building Products going forward.
04
Sequential Tuck-Ins at Favorable Multiples
Following the platform acquisition, subsequent transactions target smaller, complementary operators at multiples well below the platform's trading multiple. Kodiak Building Partners at $2.25 billion for $2.4 billion in revenue exemplifies this. The combination of sub-1.0x revenue multiple entry and cross-selling synergies to the Beacon base makes the transaction accretive to earnings in the first full year, as QXO stated at announcement.
05
Technology as a Margin Driver, Not a Product
Unlike technology companies that sell software, Jacobs uses technology to reduce the cost of operating his distribution and logistics businesses. AI-powered inventory management reduces carrying costs. Digital ordering reduces transaction friction and improves fill rates. Real-time pricing visibility reduces the margin leakage from manual negotiation. The technology investment is justified by distribution economics, not software revenue, making it more defensible and harder to replicate without the underlying scale.
06
Spin-Offs as Value Realization Events
When a platform matures, Jacobs has repeatedly created value by spinning off distinct business segments into focused pure-play vehicles. GXO and RXO were both carved out of XPO with combined spin-off values exceeding $12 billion. Each spin-off creates a re-rating event as the market assigns dedicated management focus and pure-play multiples to each separated business. The sum of the parts exceeds the conglomerate whole, and the process recycles capital and management attention into the next venture.
